# FCC Repositioning: Companion Care First

## The Shift

Instead of leading with high-acuity hospital discharge, lead with companion care — the long-term, relationship-based care that millions of families need but can't find reliably.

Hospital discharge becomes a channel and an upsell, not the core business.

---

## Why Companion Care Is the Right Starting Point

### 1. The cooperative model matters MORE in companion care
- Companion care relationships last months or years
- The product IS the relationship — the same person, the same trust, the same routine
- 77% turnover destroys this. 15% turnover enables it.
- In post-discharge care (4-8 weeks), turnover matters less because the engagement is short
- In companion care (6-36+ months), turnover is EVERYTHING

### 2. The market is larger and more accessible
- Hospital discharges needing home care: thousands per year in Boulder County
- Families with aging parents needing some level of companion support: tens of thousands in Boulder County alone
- 93M family caregivers in the US, most of whom have no professional support
- 90% of seniors want to age at home
- Most families don't need medical care — they need someone reliable

### 3. Lower barriers to entry
- Colorado Class B license (companion/personal care): $870/yr, 4-6 month process
- No skilled nursing requirement
- No clinical staff needed
- Caregiver training: topic-specific upon hire, no formal certification required
- Class A license (medical home care): much more complex — add this later as FCC grows

### 4. Better unit economics for starting small
- Lower caregiver cost (companion care doesn't require medical training)
- Longer client engagements = higher lifetime value
- Lower client acquisition cost (word of mouth in neighborhoods)
- Lower operating overhead (no clinical supervision required)

### 5. Natural expansion path
- Start: Companion care (Class B) — reliable humans showing up
- Add: Personal care services (bathing, dressing, mobility — still Class B)
- Then: Light health monitoring (medication management, vitals — may need Class A)
- Later: Post-discharge transition care (Class A, health system partnerships)
- Eventually: Full home health with skilled nursing

## Revised Value Proposition by Audience

### For Families
**Before:** "Save 2/3 vs a nursing home at $3,500/mo"
**After:** "Your mom deserves someone she knows and trusts. Starting at $550/month, get a dedicated companion caregiver who shows up consistently, knows her routine, and is invested in her wellbeing — because they own the company."

### For Health Systems
**Before:** "We solve your discharge crisis with dedicated caregivers"
**After:** "We're Boulder's trusted companion care cooperative. When your patients' families need ongoing support after discharge, we're the reliable hand-off. And because our caregivers are cooperative owners, they don't quit — so the referral you make today holds up 6 months from now."

### For Employers
**Before:** "Your caregiving employees cost you $5,365/yr"
**After:** (mostly the same, but emphasize flexibility) "Offer your caregiving employees access to reliable, affordable companion care starting at $550/mo — HSA-eligible, with caregivers who actually stay. Flexible hours that scale with what families actually need."

### For Caregivers
**Before:** (mostly the same)
**After:** "Choose your hours. Serve families in your neighborhood. Earn $25-28/hr with full benefits and equity ownership. Build real relationships with the people you care for — not a different house every week."

### For Investors/Grants
**Before:** "We solve the $2,500/day blocked bed problem"
**After:** "The $107B US home care market is broken by a single structural problem: 77% annual turnover. We fix the incentive structure through cooperative ownership. Starting with companion care — the highest-LTV, lowest-barrier segment — and expanding into medical home care as we prove the retention thesis."

## Revised Pricing Model

### Flexible, Not Fixed

| Tier | Hours/Week | Monthly Cost | Description |
|------|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| Peace of Mind | 5 hrs | ~$550 | Weekly check-in, light errands, companionship |
| Regular Support | 10-15 hrs | $1,100-1,650 | Meals, transportation, medication reminders, companionship |
| Daily Living | 20-25 hrs | $2,200-2,750 | Full companion care, personal care assistance |
| Intensive | 30-40 hrs | $3,300-4,400 | Post-hospital transition, complex needs, temporary or ongoing |

**Base rate:** $25-28/hr for companion care (below Boulder agency average of $29-45/hr)
**HSA eligibility:** Adds 25-37% effective savings through tax advantage
**No contracts:** Families scale up or down as needs change

### Why the pricing works:
- Companion caregivers don't need medical training → lower cost basis
- Cooperative model eliminates agency margin extraction → savings passed to families AND caregivers
- Traditional agency: charges family $33/hr, pays caregiver $16/hr (52% take rate)
- FCC: charges family $27/hr, pays caregiver $22-25/hr (cooperative overhead ~$2-5/hr)

## Revised Go-to-Market

### Phase 1: Companion Care (Months 1-6)
- Get Class B license from CDPHE
- Recruit founding caregiver cohort (5 people)
- Target: 10-20 families in Boulder wanting companion care
- Primary channel: direct-to-family (word of mouth, website, local marketing)
- Secondary channel: employer benefit pilot (1 employer)

### Phase 2: Add Personal Care + Health System Channel (Months 6-12)
- Expand service offerings under Class B (bathing, dressing, mobility assistance)
- Begin health system conversations (now you have a track record to show)
- BCH pitch shifts to: "We already serve 30 Boulder families. Here's our retention data. Want to be a referral partner?"
- Target: 30-50 families, 10-15 caregivers

### Phase 3: Medical Home Care + Discharge Partnerships (Year 2+)
- Apply for Class A license
- Add skilled nursing / medical caregivers
- Formalize health system discharge partnerships
- Apply for CMS ACCESS / MAHA ELEVATE

### Why this sequence is better:
- You prove the retention thesis with companion care FIRST
- When you go to health systems, you have DATA, not just a thesis
- When you apply for CMS programs, you have a track record
- The co-op has revenue and members before you take on clinical complexity

## What This Means for the BCH Conversation

The BCH conversation STILL happens, but the framing shifts:

**Old framing:** "I'm building a home care cooperative to solve your discharge crisis. Can I learn about your blocked bed problem?"

**New framing:** "I'm building a companion care cooperative in Boulder — worker-owned, focused on long-term relationships with aging adults who want to stay home. I think there's a natural connection to your discharge planning down the road, but first I want to understand: when families come to you looking for ongoing care support for their aging parents, where do you send them? Are you happy with those options?"

This is a softer, more honest entry point. You're not claiming to solve their clinical problem before you have a clinical offering. You're building a neighborhood resource that health systems will naturally want to partner with once it's proven.

## Updated Financial Implications

### Lower startup cost
- Class B license: simpler than Class A
- No clinical staff: lower initial payroll
- Companion caregivers: easier to recruit
- Smaller initial team: 5 caregivers serving 15-20 families

### Longer ramp but more sustainable
- Year 1 revenue may be lower (companion care at $25-28/hr vs $30/hr assumed before)
- But client retention is higher (companion relationships last longer)
- And expansion into medical home care is a GROWTH story for investors

### Seed round may be smaller
- $150K-250K could be sufficient for companion care launch
- vs $250K-400K for medical home care infrastructure
- Smaller ask = easier to raise = faster to market
