# The Community Care Partnership

**How a Hospital, a School District, and a Worker-Owned Cooperative Can Build Care Infrastructure That Costs Almost Nothing and Changes Everything**

*A White Paper by co-op.care Technologies LLC*
*March 2026 · Boulder, Colorado*

---

## Executive Summary

Boulder Community Health (BCH) loses an estimated $2.5 million or more annually to blocked hospital beds — patients medically ready for discharge but with nowhere safe to go. Boulder Valley School District (BVSD) loses an estimated $3.2 million annually in hidden workforce costs because 23% of its employees are managing a caregiving crisis at home. Neither institution has the resources, mandate, or expertise to solve these problems alone.

This paper proposes a Community Care Partnership in which BCH, BVSD, and co-op.care — a worker-owned home care cooperative — combine existing assets to create care infrastructure at near-zero incremental cost to either institution. The model generates new Medicare revenue for BCH, reduces workforce losses for BVSD, creates living-wage jobs with equity for caregivers, saves families $10,000+ annually through HSA/FSA tax optimization, and positions Boulder as a founding site for the CMS LEAD Model — a 10-year, $50 billion federal initiative explicitly designed for this kind of partnership.

The model is replicable. Any community with a hospital, a school district, and the will to organize a cooperative can do this.

## Part 1: The Three Problems No One Is Solving

### 1.1 The Hospital's Problem: Blocked Beds

When a patient is medically ready for discharge but has no safe home care arrangement, they remain in the hospital. In the industry, this is called a "blocked bed" or "delayed discharge."

The national average cost of a hospital bed day is $3,130 (AHA 2025). At BCH, a 303-bed community hospital handling approximately 17,000 discharges annually, even modest discharge delays create enormous costs:

| Scenario | Daily Cost | Monthly Cost | Annual Cost |
|----------|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| 5 blocked beds/day | $15,650 | $469,500 | $5.7M |
| 3 blocked beds/day | $9,390 | $281,700 | $3.4M |
| 2 blocked beds/day | $6,260 | $187,800 | $2.3M |

These are beds that cannot accept new patients from the emergency department. They create a cascade: ER wait times increase, ambulance diversions rise, scheduled surgeries are delayed, and patient satisfaction scores decline. The UK's NHS estimated that delayed discharges cost over £2 billion ($2.5 billion) annually across the system, with an average of 13,000 patients per day occupying beds they no longer need.

The root cause in most cases is not clinical — it is infrastructural. The patient is ready. There is simply no one available to care for them at home, or no coordination mechanism to arrange it quickly enough.

**BCH's current options are limited.** Traditional home care agencies have 77% annual turnover. Families scramble to find care on their own, often taking days or weeks. Patients remain hospitalized, accruing costs and acquiring hospital-associated risks (falls, infections, deconditioning) that further increase downstream spending.

### 1.2 The School District's Problem: The Invisible Workforce Crisis

BVSD employs approximately 6,000 people — teachers, administrators, custodians, bus drivers, cafeteria workers, paraprofessionals. National data from AARP (2025) shows that 73% of all employees have some caregiving responsibility. Among BVSD's workforce, an estimated 23% — roughly 1,380 people — are managing a significant caregiving situation at home.

These employees are the sandwich generation: managing aging parents while raising children and working full-time. The data on what this costs employers is stark:

| Metric | Data | Source |
|--------|------|--------|
| Employees who are caregivers | 73% have some responsibility | Harvard Business Review |
| Employees with significant caregiving | 23% | AARP/NAC 2025 |
| Cost to employer per caregiver-employee | $5,365/year | MetLife Mature Market Institute |
| Caregivers who've taken leave or demotion | 1 in 5 | Guardian |
| Working caregivers under 50 scoring "in the red" | 34% | ARCHANGELS 2025 |
| Working caregivers who feel supported | Only 32% | ARCHANGELS 2025 |

For BVSD, 1,380 caregiving employees × $5,365 in hidden costs (absenteeism, presenteeism, turnover, leave) = **$7.4 million in annual workforce impact.** Even if only half of these employees experience measurable productivity loss, the cost exceeds $3.2 million per year.

**BVSD cannot solve this directly.** The district doesn't operate a home care agency. Its benefits package doesn't include caregiver support. Its wellness programs don't address the specific needs of the sandwich generation. And its budget constraints don't allow for significant new spending.

### 1.3 The Community's Problem: The Care Gap

Behind both institutional problems is a human one. Boulder County, like every community in America, faces a demographic collision:

- **Aging population:** By 2035, 1 in 5 Americans will be over 65. In Boulder County, the over-65 population is growing faster than the state average.
- **Shrinking caregiver ratio:** The ratio of potential caregivers to every person over 80 is falling from 7:1 to 4:1 by 2030.
- **Workforce shortage:** 8.9 million direct care job openings are projected through 2032 nationally, but median pay is $16.78/hour — barely above fast food. 59% of home care workers receive public assistance.
- **Industry failure:** 77% annual caregiver turnover at agencies. $6,200/month for assisted living. 800+ nursing homes closed in the last decade. 70% of Americans who will need long-term care have no insurance for it.
- **Family burden:** 93 million Americans provide unpaid care. 51% feel stressed or depressed. 47% have no time for themselves. The average caregiver is 50 years old and working full-time.

The professional care system cannot scale fast enough. The family system is breaking. And the gap between the two is where people fall.

## Part 2: The Community Care Partnership Model

### 2.1 The Structure

The Community Care Partnership connects three existing institutions — each contributing assets they already have — through a worker-owned cooperative that provides the care delivery infrastructure none of them can build alone.

| Partner | What They Contribute | What They Receive |
|---------|---------------------|-------------------|
| **BCH (Hospital)** | Discharge referrals via PointClickCare ADT alerts. Clinical data. Existing relationships with post-acute patients. | Freed beds ($3,130/day saved per bed). New Medicare revenue via CHI/PIN billing ($85–120/patient/month). Reduced 30-day readmission rates. LEAD Model qualification. |
| **BVSD (School District)** | Employee benefit enrollment ($3–6/employee/month from existing wellness budget). Trust and community network (every family in Boulder is connected to BVSD). | Reduced absenteeism and presenteeism. Lower turnover among caregiving employees. Measurable ROI: $5,365 in hidden costs vs. $36–72/year per employee. |
| **co-op.care (Cooperative)** | Care delivery infrastructure: W-2 caregivers ($25–28/hr + equity), physician-directed care plans, CII/CRI assessment tools, CareOS technology platform, Time Bank, caregiver training pathways. | Referral pipeline (hospital discharges + employer benefit enrollees). Clinical data for outcome measurement. Community trust via institutional partnerships. Membership revenue + Medicare billing + employer benefit fees. |

### 2.2 The Cost to Each Partner

**Cost to BCH: Near zero.** BCH doesn't pay co-op.care for discharge coordination. BCH refers patients through its existing ADT notification system (PointClickCare/Collective Medical, which BCH already uses). co-op.care bills Medicare directly for CHI (Community Health Integration, G0019/G0022) and PIN (Principal Illness Navigation, G0023/G0024) services at $85–120 per patient per month. BCH saves $3,130 per blocked bed day freed. The net financial impact for BCH is strongly positive from day one.

**Cost to BVSD: $3–6 per employee per month.** This comes from existing wellness budget allocation — comparable to what districts spend on EAP (Employee Assistance Programs) or wellness app subscriptions. For 6,000 employees at $4/month average, BVSD's total cost is $288,000/year. Against $3.2 million+ in hidden caregiving costs, the ROI is 11:1.

**Cost to co-op.care: Operating costs funded by revenue.** co-op.care's operating costs (caregiver wages, benefits, technology, insurance) are funded by three revenue streams flowing directly from the partnership: family private pay ($35/hour, HSA/FSA eligible), Medicare CHI/PIN billing ($85–120/patient/month), and employer benefit fees ($3–6/employee/month). No grant funding, no subsidy, and no charitable contribution required from either institutional partner.

### 2.3 How the Money Flows

The most important feature of this model is that the money flows are self-sustaining. No one is subsidizing anyone else.

**Flow 1: Hospital discharge → co-op.care → Medicare**
1. BCH patient is medically ready for discharge
2. PointClickCare ADT alert notifies co-op.care in real time
3. co-op.care caregiver coordinates home transition (same day or next day)
4. co-op.care physician (Josh Emdur, DO — BCH hospitalist since 2008) reviews care plan
5. co-op.care bills Medicare directly for CHI/PIN services ($85–120/month)
6. BCH frees the bed, avoids $3,130/day cost, and reduces 30-day readmission risk

**Flow 2: BVSD employee benefit → co-op.care → family member**
1. BVSD offers co-op.care as an employee benefit (like EAP)
2. Employee with aging parent enrolls in Care Plan ($59/month, paid by employee, HSA/FSA eligible)
3. co-op.care physician reviews care plan for the employee's parent
4. HSA/FSA eligibility saves the employee 28–36% on care costs — $10,000+/year
5. Employee returns to work less stressed, less absent, more productive
6. BVSD's hidden caregiving costs decrease

**Flow 3: Time Bank → community → community**
1. Neighbor gives an hour (drives someone to pharmacy, sits with a patient after surgery)
2. Neighbor earns a Time Bank credit worth $35
3. Neighbor redeems the credit when they need help (or banks it for the future)
4. No money changes hands. No institutional cost. Pure community reciprocity.
5. The Congressional Budget Office estimates family members provide 55% of care keeping elders out of nursing homes. The Time Bank makes that invisible labor visible and exchangeable.

### 2.4 The Physician Governance Mechanism

The entire model is anchored by physician oversight. Josh Emdur, DO serves as co-op.care's Medical Director and has been a BCH hospitalist since 2008. His role creates three structural advantages:

1. **HSA/FSA eligibility:** His Letter of Medical Necessity converts custodial home care into a qualified medical expense under IRS Publication 502, Section 213(d). This saves each family 28–36% on care costs. No traditional agency can offer this.

2. **Medicare billing authority:** His physician attestation enables CHI/PIN billing ($85–120/patient/month), which funds the care coordination that makes hospital discharge work.

3. **Clinical quality assurance:** Every care plan is physician-reviewed. Every CII/CRI assessment is validated. The 4-layer safety model (Clinical Screening, Identity Verification, Care History Cross-Check, Physician Oversight) exceeds the clinical governance of any traditional agency.

### 2.5 What the Families Experience

The Alpha Daughter — a 50-year-old BVSD teacher managing her aging mother's care — experiences this:

- She enrolls through her BVSD employee benefit
- She completes a CRI-14 assessment for her mother (15 minutes, online or by phone)
- co-op.care matches her mother with a caregiver based on needs, personality, and schedule
- The physician reviews the care plan and signs the Letter of Medical Necessity
- She submits the LMN to her HSA administrator
- Her $35/hour care is now paid with pre-tax dollars, saving her $10,366/year
- The same caregiver shows up every Tuesday and Thursday
- Her mother is safe. She goes to work. She stops crying in the car.

## Part 3: The LEAD Model — Federal Funding for This Exact Partnership

### 3.1 What Is the LEAD Model?

On December 18, 2025, CMS announced the Long-term Enhanced ACO Design (LEAD) Model — the most ambitious accountable care initiative in federal history. LEAD is a 10-year voluntary program (2027–2036) that pays for coordinated, outcome-aligned care — exactly what the Community Care Partnership delivers.

Key features that align with this model:

| LEAD Feature | Community Care Partnership Application |
|-------------|---------------------------------------|
| **10-year performance period** | Long enough to build genuine community infrastructure, not just a pilot |
| **Focus on homebound/home-limited patients** | co-op.care's primary population: aging adults who want to stay home |
| **Prospective, capitated payments** | Predictable cash flow for the cooperative instead of fee-for-service uncertainty |
| **Smaller/independent provider inclusion** | co-op.care is exactly the kind of small, community-based organization LEAD targets |
| **Medicaid integration** | CMS will select 2 states for ACO-Medicaid partnerships (planning phase March 2026–Dec 2027). Colorado should be one. |
| **CARA episode-based risk arrangements** | BCH and co-op.care can establish formal risk-sharing for discharge episodes, including a falls prevention program |
| **Benefit Enhancements** | Expanded Medical Nutrition Therapy, healthy living supports — directly applicable to in-home care |
| **Reduced attribution minimums** | Makes participation feasible for a community cooperative serving a single county |

### 3.2 Why Boulder Should Be a LEAD Site

The LEAD Model application opens March 2026. Boulder has unique advantages:

1. **Josh Emdur, DO** is both co-op.care's Medical Director and a BCH hospitalist. He bridges the hospital and the cooperative organically — not through a vendor contract.

2. **IAALS at the University of Denver** designed the Utah regulatory sandbox model. A co-op.care sandbox pilot, evaluated by IAALS, gives CMS an independent academic partner for the LEAD site.

3. **Colorado's cooperative infrastructure** — RMEOC, Legacy Exit, SB24-205's 75% cooperative conversion tax credit — provides a statewide pipeline for replication.

4. **The CMS Medicaid integration planning phase** (March 2026–Dec 2027) is explicitly seeking states interested in ACO-Medicaid partnerships. Colorado, with its Medicaid expansion and HCPF Rural Health Transformation work, is a natural candidate.

5. **PointClickCare/Collective Medical ADT integration** across 1,300+ hospitals means the discharge coordination mechanism works nationally from day one — not just at BCH.

### 3.3 The LEAD Financial Model for Boulder

Under LEAD, the Community Care Partnership generates revenue through multiple channels simultaneously:

|---------------|------------------------|--------|
| Prospective capitated payments (LEAD) | $500K–$1.2M | Federal (CMS) |
| Medicare CHI/PIN billing | $306K | Federal (Medicare) |
| Family private pay ($35/hr, HSA/FSA) | $3.3M | Families (pre-tax) |

Of this revenue, approximately 70% ($3.2M–$3.8M) flows directly to caregiver wages and benefits. Under cooperative ownership, this money stays in Boulder. Co-ops recirculate 2.6× more money locally than corporate businesses. The Community Care Partnership doesn't just deliver care — it builds community wealth.

## Part 4: Why This Model Is Replicable

### 4.1 The Three Universal Assets

Every community in America has:

1. **A hospital** that loses money to blocked beds and readmissions
2. **An employer** (school district, municipality, large local business) with a workforce affected by caregiving
3. **Neighbors** who are already providing unpaid care but have no infrastructure to organize it

The Community Care Partnership connects these three assets through a cooperative structure that costs the hospital nothing, costs the employer $3–6/employee/month, and funds itself through Medicare billing + family private pay + employer benefit fees.

### 4.2 What the Cooperative Provides

The cooperative is the missing infrastructure layer. It contributes:

- **W-2 caregivers** at $25–28/hour with benefits and equity (vs. $13–17 with nothing at traditional agencies)
- **Physician oversight** enabling HSA/FSA eligibility and Medicare billing
- **Technology** (CareOS platform, FHIR R4, CII/CRI assessments) for care coordination and outcome measurement
- **Time Bank** for neighbor-to-neighbor care exchange
- **Training pathways** (CNA → LPN → RN) with tuition assistance
- **Insurance** (Beazley Virtual Care via Lloyd's of London)
- **AI validation** (Automate Clinic Reality.check™) ensuring clinical safety

### 4.3 The co-op.care Federation Model

co-op.care provides the technology, clinical governance, and playbooks. Each local cooperative is 100% community-owned. The "national brain, local hands" federation model means:

- Any community can apply to start a cooperative
- They receive a turnkey package: CareOS, training, insurance, playbooks
- They own their cooperative independently
- They share data (anonymized) to improve the system for everyone
- The federation captures a percentage of insurance premiums for sustainability

### 4.4 The Replication Formula

| Step | Action | Timeline | Cost |
|------|--------|----------|------|
| 1 | Identify hospital partner willing to share ADT alerts | Month 1 | $0 |
| 2 | Identify employer partner willing to offer as benefit | Month 1–2 | $0 |
| 3 | Recruit founding caregiver-owners (5–10 to start) | Month 2–3 | Training costs only |

**Total startup cost for a community: Under $50,000** — primarily caregiver training and licensing fees. The hospital and employer contribute assets they already have (referrals, benefits budget) at no incremental cost. Federal revenue (Medicare, LEAD, ACCESS) begins flowing within 12 months.

### 5.2 The Cooperative Multiplier

Worker-owned cooperatives recirculate 2.6× more money locally than equivalent corporate businesses. When a co-op.care caregiver earns $28/hour in Boulder, she spends it in Boulder — rent, groceries, childcare, local businesses. When a PE-owned agency pays $14/hour and sends the rest to headquarters in Dallas, that money leaves.

At $3.5M in annual caregiver wages, the local economic multiplier generates roughly $9.1M in total local economic activity versus $3.5M under a corporate agency model. The difference — $5.6M in additional local circulation — is the cooperative advantage expressed in real dollars.
