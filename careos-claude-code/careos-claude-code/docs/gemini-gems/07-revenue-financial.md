# co-op.care — Revenue Model & Financial Framework

---

## REVENUE LAYERS (by margin, highest first)

### Layer 1: PACE Sub-capitation (Highest Margin)

**What it is:** TRU PACE (Lafayette, CO) receives capitated monthly payments from CMS for each enrolled dual-eligible senior. co-op.care sub-contracts as the home care delivery partner, receiving a portion of that capitation for home-based services.

**Why highest margin:** Payment is capitated (fixed monthly per member), not fee-for-service. The cooperative controls utilization through proactive care, family caregiver training, and community wellness referrals. Every hospitalization prevented and every care hour shifted to trained family members improves margin.

**Key economics:**
- PACE capitation rates vary by state and acuity but typically range $3,000–$5,000+/month per enrollee
- co-op.care's sub-capitation share would be negotiated based on the scope of services delivered
- Margin improvement levers: caregiver training reducing paid care hours, community wellness referrals reducing medical interventions, continuity reducing hospitalizations

**Timeline:** November 2026 pitch to TRU PACE is Tier 1 milestone.

**Target partner:** TRU PACE, Lafayette, Colorado.

---

### Layer 2: LEAD/CARA Institutional Revenue

**What it is:** CMS payment models (expected 2027) designed to pay for home and community-based services that keep people out of institutional care settings.

**Strategic fit:** Directly aligned with co-op.care's entire model — the cooperative exists to keep people home. These models would pay co-op.care for doing exactly what it's already doing.

**Timeline:** 2027 eligibility window. Regulatory details still evolving.

---

### Layer 3: Employer Partnerships (Primary Growth Channel)

**What it is:** Companies purchase co-op.care membership as an employee benefit. Framed as "Age at Home Care Insurance" — an alternative to traditional Long-Term Care Insurance (LTCI).

**Why primary growth channel:** 
- The Alpha Daughter is employed. Her employer has a direct interest in her productivity, which eldercare responsibility destroys.
- Employer-sponsored benefits provide predictable, recurring membership revenue
- B2B sales cycles are longer but yield bulk member acquisition
- Positions co-op.care as a benefits infrastructure company, not just a care provider

**Pricing framework:** Membership fee per employee/family (specific pricing to be finalized based on founding member economics and actuarial analysis).

**Comparison to LTCI:**
- LTCI: expensive, exclusionary underwriting, pays only after significant disability, episodic
- co-op.care: affordable membership, no underwriting exclusions, starts at wellness tier, continuous relationship

---

### Layer 4: Direct Membership (Founding Deposits → Ongoing)

**What it is:** Individual families join co-op.care directly through the founding deposit process and ongoing membership.

**Founding mechanics:**
- $100 refundable deposit per family
- 200 founding families required to trigger Boulder County launch
- Deposits validate demand and create community ownership before operations begin

**Ongoing membership:** Monthly or annual membership fees (specific pricing TBD based on market validation and actuarial work).

---

### Layer 5: Time Bank Economics

**What it is:** Internal currency system where members earn credits through cooperative participation and redeem them for care services.

**Revenue implications:**
- 12-month graduated credit expiry creates natural credit decay (prevents hoarding, encourages circulation)
- Comfort Card serves as reconciliation instrument
- Time Bank reduces cash outlay for some care hours while increasing community engagement
- Not a primary revenue source but reduces cost of care delivery and strengthens cooperative bonds

## THREE-SCENARIO FINANCIAL PROJECTIONS

The investor deck includes three scenarios. Key variables across scenarios:

**Conservative:**
- Slower founding family acquisition
- PACE sub-capitation delayed or smaller scope
- Employer partnerships take longer to close
- Break-even timeline extended

**Base:**
- 200 founding families on schedule
- TRU PACE sub-capitation secured in 2027
- 2–3 employer partnerships in Year 1
- Break-even within projected timeline

**Aggressive:**
- Founding families exceed 200
- TRU PACE sub-capitation plus additional PACE partners
- 5+ employer partnerships in Year 1
- LEAD/CARA revenue captured early
- Federation expansion to second city in Year 2

**Key metrics tracked across all scenarios:**
- Member acquisition cost (by channel: direct, employer, PACE)
- Lifetime member value
- Caregiver utilization rate (paid hours / available hours)
- Margin by revenue layer
- Caregiver retention rate (cooperative W2 model vs. industry average)
- Hospitalization rate for members vs. comparable population
- Time-to-break-even

## COST STRUCTURE

**Major cost categories:**
- Caregiver wages and benefits (W2 — the largest cost, and deliberately so)
- Medical Director compensation (Dr. Emdur)
- Technology (CareOS development and hosting)
- Member acquisition (marketing, outreach, sales)
- Training (caregiver certification, family caregiver training program development)
- Federation administration
- Insurance and compliance

**Cost advantage of cooperative model:**
- W2 caregivers have lower turnover than 1099 contractors (industry turnover ~65–80%; cooperative target significantly lower)
- Lower turnover = lower recruiting/training costs per care hour delivered
- Family caregiver training shifts some care hours from paid to unpaid (with member consent)
- Community wellness referrals reduce medical care intensity
- Continuity reduces expensive care transitions and hospitalizations
