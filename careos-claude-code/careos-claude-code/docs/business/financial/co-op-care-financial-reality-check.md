# co-op.care Financial Reality Check
## Actuarial Data at Scale — March 2026

### Sources & Methodology
All figures below cite published 2025–2026 data. Projections use 30% pessimism buffers on revenue and 20% optimism buffers on costs (i.e., we assume we'll earn 30% less and spend 20% more than the base case).

## 1. Market Rate Reality — What Families Actually Pay

### National Data (2025–2026)
| Source | National Median Hourly | Colorado Hourly |
|--------|----------------------|-----------------|
| A Place for Mom 2025 | $33/hr (nonmedical) | Not listed separately |
| Genworth 2026 Survey | $31–35/hr (homemaker to HHA) | ~$33/hr |
| Care.com (Jan 2026) | $22.47 starting (caregiver-posted) | $24.89 starting (Denver) |
| Elder Answers 2025 | $25–35/hr range | $22–29/hr |
| Senioridy 2026 | $31/hr (national personal care) | ~$30–33/hr |

**Key insight:** The national median charged TO families is $31–35/hr. The average posted rate BY caregivers (what they ask for as independents) is $22–25. The spread between what families pay and what caregivers earn is the extraction zone.

### co-op.care at $35/hr: Where Does This Sit?
- **At or slightly above national median.** Not premium. Not discount. Market rate.
- **Competitive in Boulder County** where cost of living is above Colorado average
- **Below most Denver agency rates** of $30–40+/hr for personal care
- **Above the Care.com independent rate** of $25 — but we're offering W-2 employment, benefits, and physician oversight

### What Families Should Know (website-appropriate)
> "$35 per hour is market rate for home care in the Boulder area. The difference is where the money goes. At a traditional agency, your caregiver sees $13–17 of that. Here, they see $25–28. The rest covers benefits, training, insurance, and operations. Nobody extracts a profit."

## 2. Industry Cost Structure — Where the Money Actually Goes

### Traditional Agency Breakdown ($35/hr billed)
| Cost Category | % of Revenue | $/hr |
|--------------|-------------|------|
| **Caregiver wages** | 38–48% | $13–17 |
| Payroll taxes & workers comp | 8–12% | $3–4 |
| Benefits (if any) | 0–3% | $0–1 |
| Admin/office staff | 8–12% | $3–4 |
| Marketing & client acquisition | 3–5% | $1–2 |
| Insurance (liability, E&O) | 2–3% | $0.70–1 |
| Office/rent/software | 3–5% | $1–2 |
| **Owner profit / PE extraction** | **15–30%** | **$5–10** |

**The extraction zone: $5–10 per hour goes to owners/investors who provide no care.**

### co-op.care Breakdown ($35/hr billed)
| Cost Category | % of Revenue | $/hr | Notes |
|--------------|-------------|------|-------|
| **Caregiver wages** | 71–80% | $25–28 | W-2, not 1099 |
| Payroll taxes (FICA, FUTA, SUTA) | 7.65–8.5% | $2.70–3.00 | Employer share |
| Workers compensation | 2–3% | $0.70–1.05 | CO home care class code |
| Health benefits | 3–5% | $1.05–1.75 | ACA-compliant group plan |
| **Equity contribution** | 2–3% | $0.70–1.05 | ~$10,400/yr per FT caregiver |
| Admin/operations | 3–5% | $1.05–1.75 | Lean: AI-assisted scheduling, billing |
| Insurance (Beazley Virtual Care) | 1–2% | $0.35–0.70 | Professional + tech + cyber |
| Training & development | 1–2% | $0.35–0.70 | CNA/LPN pathways |
| Technology (CareOS/Aidbox) | 0.5–1% | $0.18–0.35 | FHIR R4 infrastructure |
| **Surplus/reserve** | 0–3% | $0–1.05 | Cooperative surplus, not "profit" |

**Total caregiver compensation (wages + benefits + equity): 83–91% of revenue.**
**Traditional agency caregiver compensation: 38–51% of revenue.**

### What This Means in Plain English
For every $35 a family pays:
- **Traditional agency:** $13–17 reaches the caregiver. $5–10 goes to investors.
- **co-op.care:** $25–28 reaches the caregiver as wages. Another $2–4 goes to their benefits and equity. Total caregiver value: $29–32 of every $35.

### Website-appropriate claim:
> "Depending on your tax bracket and hours of care, HSA/FSA eligibility saves your family between $6,500 and $19,700 per year. For a typical family in the 22% federal bracket using 20 hours per week, the savings are approximately $12,400 annually."

**Note:** We previously claimed "28–36% savings." The actual range by bracket is 24–44%. The 28–36% claim is accurate for the middle brackets (22–24% federal) which represent the largest share of the target demographic. The claim is honest and conservative.

### HSA/FSA Limits (2026)
- HSA family contribution limit: ~$8,550/year
- FSA contribution limit: ~$3,300/year
- DCFSA (Dependent Care FSA): $5,000/year
- **Important:** Families can use multiple accounts. HSA has no annual spending limit (only contribution limit). Unused HSA rolls over. FSA is use-it-or-lose-it.
- **Requirement:** Letter of Medical Necessity from Josh. One-time per care plan period. Quarterly renewal.

## 8. Scale Economics — What Happens at 100, 500, 1000 Members

### Unit Economics Improve with Scale
| Metric | 25 Members | 100 Members | 500 Members | 1000 Members |
|--------|-----------|-------------|-------------|--------------|
| Admin cost per member/mo | $160 | $48 | $16 | $10 |
| Insurance cost per member/mo | $27 | $12 | $6 | $4 |
| Technology per member/mo | $20 | $5 | $2 | $1.50 |
| Caregiver wages (% of care rev) | 74% | 74% | 74% | 74% |
| **Operating margin** | **5–8%** | **15–19%** | **22–25%** | **25–28%** |

### What Scale Unlocks
- **At 50 members:** Break-even. Can hire a part-time admin.
- **At 100 members:** Positive cash flow. Josh's compensation scales to $5K/mo.
- **At 200 members:** Full-time admin. Marketing budget. Start employer B2B channel.
- **At 500 members:** Insurance product feasible (enough actuarial data). First federation co-ops.
- **At 1000 members:** MGA distribution model viable. National expansion funded from operations.
