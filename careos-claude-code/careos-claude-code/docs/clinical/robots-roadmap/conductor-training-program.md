# co-op.care Conductor Training Program
## Family Caregiver Education as a Core Service Offering

---

## The Principle

The Conductor isn't just an orchestrator — she's a trained participant when she chooses to be. co-op.care trains family caregivers on any care activities they want to perform themselves. This transforms the weekend visit from helpless watching to competent participation.

Training is a revenue stream, a retention mechanism, and a clinical safety intervention.

---

## Training Modules

### Module 1: Safe Transfers ($150, 2 hours)
**What she learns:** Bed-to-chair, chair-to-standing, car transfers, toilet transfers. Proper body mechanics to protect both herself and her parent. When to use a gait belt. When NOT to attempt a transfer alone.
**Why it matters:** The #1 injury for family caregivers is back injury from improper transfers. The #1 injury for aging adults is falls during transfers. This module prevents both.
**Format:** In-person with worker-owner demonstrating on training equipment. Hands-on practice. Take-home reference card.
**HSA/FSA eligible:** Yes, under Medical Director's LMN as caregiver safety education.

### Module 2: Bathing and Personal Hygiene Assistance ($150, 2 hours)
**What she learns:** Positioning for safety, water temperature management, skin inspection during bathing, dignity preservation techniques, adaptive equipment use.
**Why it matters:** Bathing is the most intimate care task. Most family caregivers avoid it, leading to hygiene decline. Training gives confidence and preserves the parent's dignity.
**Format:** In-person demonstration. Practice with adaptive equipment. Discussion of emotional dynamics.

### Module 3: Medication Management ($200, 3 hours)
**What she learns:** Medication organizer setup, timing and interaction awareness, what to watch for (side effects, missed doses, changes in response), when to call the physician, how to communicate with the pharmacy.
**Why it matters:** Medication errors are the leading cause of preventable hospitalizations in aging adults. A trained Conductor catches errors that rotating agency staff miss.
**Format:** Classroom + hands-on with sample med organizers. Take-home medication tracking template.

### Module 4: Dementia Communication and Care ($250, 4 hours)
**What she learns:** Validation therapy basics, redirection techniques, sundowning response, managing disinhibition (when Mom says hurtful things), creating a calm environment, activity programming for cognitive engagement, wandering prevention.
**Why it matters:** Dementia is the highest-intensity caregiving situation. Untrained family members escalate behavioral episodes. Trained family members de-escalate them.
**Format:** Classroom + role-play scenarios. Video examples. Take-home quick-reference guide. Includes the community compact discussion (understanding that the person saying hurtful things isn't the person they were).

### Module 5: Fall Prevention and Home Safety ($150, 2 hours)
**What she learns:** Home safety walkthrough methodology, grab bar placement, lighting assessment, rug/clutter hazards, footwear recommendations, what to do when a fall happens (recovery positioning, when to call 911 vs. when to call the cooperative).
**Why it matters:** Falls are the most expensive preventable acute event. A trained Conductor makes the home safer during every visit.
**Format:** In-person home safety audit of member's actual home. Checklist and recommendations.

### Module 6: Nutrition for Aging ($150, 2 hours)
**What she learns:** Dysphagia awareness (choking risk), texture modification, hydration strategies, meal prep for medical diets (diabetes, heart failure, renal), recognizing malnutrition signs.
**Why it matters:** Nutrition decline is a leading indicator of overall decline. A Conductor who can cook safely for Mom's condition during visits extends the Time Bank meal support.
**Format:** Classroom + kitchen demonstration. Recipe cards for common medical diets.

### Module 7: Emergency Response Protocol ($100, 1.5 hours)
**What she learns:** When to call 911 vs. when to call the cooperative vs. when to monitor and report. How to communicate with EMTs. What information to have ready. Post-emergency documentation for the care team.
**Why it matters:** Panic is the default response. A protocol replaces panic with competence.
**Format:** Classroom + scenario walkthroughs. Laminated quick-reference card for the refrigerator.

### Module 8: Comprehensive Conductor Certification ($750, full day)
**What's included:** All seven modules compressed into an intensive 8-hour training day. Covers transfers, bathing, medications, dementia, falls, nutrition, and emergency response.
**Who it's for:** The Conductor who's about to start spending significant time providing direct care — either because she's moved closer, because she's retired, or because acuity has increased.
**Certification:** co-op.care Certified Family Caregiver. Documented in CareOS. Referenced in the care plan.
**HSA/FSA eligible:** Yes, full program under Medical Director's LMN.

## Retention Mechanism

A trained Conductor is deeply embedded in the co-op.care model:
- Her training is documented in CareOS and referenced in the care plan
- Her competencies are visible on the Conductor Dashboard
- Her physician knows what she can and can't do during visits
- Her worker-owner caregiver coordinates with her trained capabilities

Switching to a traditional agency means losing all of that integration. The training isn't transferable to an agency that doesn't know what she's been trained on or how to coordinate with her.

## Clinical Safety Argument

**For physicians (Rhett Silver, Dr. Stacy Fischer):**
"We train family caregivers on the specific tasks they'll perform during visits. A trained daughter who can safely assist with transfers is better than an untrained daughter who tries anyway and causes a fall. A trained daughter who understands dementia communication is better than one who argues with her mother's delusions. This training is physician-supervised, documented in the care plan, and coordinated with our professional caregiving team. It's not replacing professional care — it's making the family's contributions safer."
